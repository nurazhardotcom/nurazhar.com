In Why AGI Won't Happen, I argued that AGI is not a technical milestone. It is a power construct — whoever gets to define "intelligence" gets to decide who counts as intelligent, what problems matter, and which systems deserve resources.

That post was about how technology does not exist in a vacuum.

This post is about what fills the vacuum.

The Three Narratives

When you read the AGI debate from Singapore — a city-state neutral by necessity — you see not one debate but three competing civilizational narratives, each with its own definition of intelligence.

1. The Western Narrative: Intelligence as Universal Scalability

This is Silicon Valley's AGI. It assumes:

  • Intelligence is a property of information processing, separable from embodiment
  • A sufficiently large neural network will eventually replicate any human cognitive function
  • The goal is to scale compute until "general" emerges
  • Intelligence is universal — the same in a human, a machine, or an alien

This narrative descends from the Enlightenment: reason is universal, truth is objective, and the individual rational mind is the unit of intelligence. It is not coincidental that the companies driving this narrative are overwhelmingly American and European.

The political consequence: if intelligence is universal and scalable, then the civilization that scales it fastest wins. AGI becomes a race — and the winner gets to define what "winning" means.

2. The Chinese Narrative: Intelligence as Harmony and Governance

China's AI strategy assumes something different:

  • Intelligence serves social harmony, not individual cognition
  • A system is intelligent to the extent it maintains stability, optimizes collective outcomes, and aligns with governance objectives
  • The unit of intelligence is the society, not the individual
  • Western "general intelligence" looks like chaos disguised as freedom

This is not a misunderstanding of Western AGI. It is a competing definition rooted in Confucian and Legalist traditions where the highest form of intelligence is not reasoning but ordering — arranging people, resources, and information into a functioning whole.

The political consequence: if intelligence is social harmony, then AGI is not a product you build but a governance structure you achieve. The "AGI race" is not about compute but about civilizational coherence.

3. The Third Position: Intelligence as Relation

Between these two poles, there is a third position — less articulated, but no less real.

In several intellectual traditions outside the Western and Chinese orbits, reason is not separable from relational wisdom. Intelligence is not what you can compute but how well you navigate relationship — with people, with the natural world, and between competing values.

This tradition does not produce AGI companies or national AI strategies. It produces a question that neither the Western nor the Chinese narrative can answer:

What is intelligence for?

If the answer is not "to maximize compute" or "to optimize governance" but something older — to live well together — then both AGI projects look like they are asking the wrong question.

Why the Competing Worldview Matters

AGI is usually discussed as a technical problem: can we build a machine that thinks like a human?

But the word "think" is doing all the work. Every civilization has a different answer to what thinking is:

TraditionThinking is...AGI would be...
Western EnlightenmentRational information processingA universal reasoning engine
Chinese ConfucianOrdering relationships harmoniouslyA governance optimization system
Relational traditionsNavigating relation with wisdomA companion, not a sovereign
IndianTranscending illusion (Maya)A system that sees through the veil
IndigenousStewardship of land and kinEmbedded in place, not scalable

None of these is wrong. They are incommensurable. You cannot build one AGI that satisfies all of them because they disagree on what intelligence is for.

The implication: the AGI race is not a race to a finish line. It is a race to impose a definition.

The civilization that defines intelligence gets to decide that its values are universal. That is the real prize — not the technology, but the authority to say what counts.

The Singaporean Position

Singapore is Chinese-majority, multi-ethnic, English-speaking, and neutral by design. It has no civilizational project to impose. It survives by being useful to everyone and captured by no one.

From this position, the AGI debate looks different:

  • The Western narrative feels familiar (English, markets, universities) but naive about power
  • The Chinese narrative feels familiar (hierarchy, community, pragmatic governance) but uncomfortable in its scale
  • Neither narrative speaks to the experience of being a minority in someone else's civilizational project

This is not a weakness. It is the exact position needed to see that AGI is a worldview competition rather than a technology race. When you are not captured by either pole, you can observe the shape of the contest itself.

That decision is being made right now — in datacenter locations, training data provenance, model alignment choices, and conference keynote speeches. Every technical choice encodes a civilizational preference.

Technology does not live in a vacuum. The vacuum is filled by whoever has the power to define the terms.
